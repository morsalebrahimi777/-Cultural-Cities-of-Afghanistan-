Herat

Herat is one of the oldest and most cultural cities in Afghanistan, located in the west of the country and the capital of Herat Province. The city is famous for its Islamic architecture, art, poetry, and ancient history. Herat was once part of the Silk Road and is still one of the important economic and cultural centers of Afghanistan today.

Kabul

Kabul, the capital of Afghanistan, is the largest city in the country, located in the eastern part of Afghanistan and in the valleys between the mountains. It is the political, cultural, and economic center of Afghanistan.

Mazar-e-Sharif

Mazar-e-Sharif is one of the largest and most important cities in northern Afghanistan and is the capital of Balkh Province. The city is famous for the holy shrine of Imam Ali (AS) and hosts thousands of pilgrims every year. Mazar-e-Sharif is of great cultural, historical and economic importance and has been relatively quieter than some other parts of Afghanistan.

Bamyan

Bamiyan, Afghanistan The Bamiyan statues were massive, ancient Buddhist statues carved into the rocks of Bamiyan, in central Afghanistan. Dating back to the 6th century AD, they were among the largest Buddha statues in the world. The Taliban destroyed them in 2001, but their remains still stand as a symbol of Afghanistan's rich cultural history.

Customs and traditions in Afghanistan are a combination of different traditions, cultures, and beliefs that have developed in the country over the centuries. These customs are very important in the daily lives of the people of Afghanistan and vary according to the different ethnicities and regions of the country.
Afghanistan, due to its location on the Silk Road, was a crossroads for major world civilizations and was one of the most important trading centers in ancient times. This important and sensitive strategic and geopolitical position of Afghanistan has played an important role in shaping a rich mosaic of cultures and civilizations from Iran, Central Asia, the Middle East and South Asia in this country. Since ancient times and throughout historical periods, the people of Afghanistan have been a major center for the introduction and propagation of world religions and have played an important role in trade and commerce, and have been a dominant political and cultural center in Asia.

__celebrations__

Nowruz is one of the oldest festivals left over from ancient times. The time of Nowruz is at the beginning of spring. In Afghanistan, as well as in Iran, Nowruz is considered the beginning of the New Year. This festival traditionally lasts for two weeks and people prepare for the beginning of this festival several days in advance (at least before Ash Wednesday).

Nowruz celebrations in Afghanistan include:

Red Festival

The Red Cabbage Festival (Mileh Red Cabbage Festival) is a special celebration that is held in Mazar-e-Sharif during the first 120 days of the year to mark the arrival of the red cabbages. During these 10 days, people from all over the world come to Mazar-e-Sharif to attend this celebration.

Peasant Festival

Another Nowruz celebration in Afghanistan is the "Farmer's Festival," which is held on the first day of the year. Farmers come to cities and display their produce.

Samanak

Women hold a special ceremony for Samanak (semno) during the night and are busy cooking it from late evening until the next morning.

Haft mivh

Afghanistan is known for its Haft Sin, a dish made with seven different dried fruits soaked in water. Haft Sin is a fruit salad-like dish made with seven different types of dried fruits soaked in water.

__Types of Afghan Food__

Afghanistan has delicious and diverse food, and every city in Afghanistan has its own famous and special food.

Qabili Polo

It is one of the most famous types of pilaf in Afghanistan, and almost all neighboring countries are familiar with its name. It is made from thin rice, kosht yakhni kosfand, zardak (carrot), raisins, and pistachio slices.

Kachari Qorut

It is one of the famous Afghan dishes that is mostly cooked in winter. On the first day of winter, people put a chair or stove and cook a Kachari qurot.

Ashk

It is a type of local food that is prepared using a paste of kandam and kandaneh (leer).

Manto

One of the delicious Afghan dishes that is steamed, Manto is also one of the most famous and favorite dishes of the people of Central Asia.

Types of stew:

Stews are usually a combination of vegetables, dried or fresh vegetables, and legumes such as chickpeas and beans. They are called qormeh and are usually served with bread or rice.

Qourma sabzi

It is made from red meat, onion, spinach, parsley, and beans.

Lung Stew

It is common in Herat and is made from red cabbage, onion, garlic, grated carrots, and Roman rub.

Shole Ghorbandi

The Ghorband district of Parwan province produces a special dish called "Ghorbandi Shola". This dish is made from rice, mung beans, sorghum, and kausht (a type of rice) called kaushfand.

Bolani

One of the famous Afghan dishes is to dip charred yam paste into yam or potato curd, place it in a pan in a semi-circle shape, and fry it in oil.

__Music of Afghanistan__

The music of Afghanistan is a beautiful reflection of the ethnic and linguistic diversity of Afghanistan. The country is home to a wide range of musical styles and genres, from the refined Persian ghazal to Indian rock. The music of Afghanistan is largely lyrical, although there is also a significant repertoire of non-verbal music. Poetry has a prominent place in the country's musical culture.

Throughout the thousands of years of history of this land, the Aryans have used music and dance to perform religious ceremonies. One of the earliest musical forms in this land is the Rekoda. The sounds, rhythms, and themes of the Rekoda still influence modern Afghan music.

Afghan music styles

Local music

Every ethnic group in Afghanistan has its own local musical style. The songs, regardless of where they come from, are mostly about the nightingale, the beloved, and are based on epic poetry and legends. The music of the regions and styles that are famous include: Badakhshi style, Kharabati, Qataghni, Hazarki, Dastan Samt Mashriqi, Ghazni style, Kiliwali of the Pashtun nationality, Turkmen, Herati, Qarsak Panjshir, and the harmonic or Jund Awaz Keshin of the people of Nuristan.

Musical instruments

The rabab is the national instrument of Afghanistan and is a short-necked stringed instrument played with the hands or a bow. Stringed instruments with long necks, such as the tanbur, dutar, and dambur, are also common, as are stringed instruments with curved necks, such as the sarinda and ghijak. The tanbur and dutar are the only instruments unique to Afghanistan.

__Local Danses__

The Atan or Atan Melli dance is one of the national dances of Afghanistan. This dance was initially popular among the Pashtun people living in Afghanistan and Pakistan . The Atan Melli is usually performed collectively by young men or women. The Atan Melli dance is performed in a circular manner with regular head and hand movements. It is also believed that this dance was popularized during the Greek period in the ancient land of Bactria in northern Afghanistan.

Panjshiri Korsak dance

This dance is popular among the Tajik people living in the Janjjir region and is performed while beating the hands and making a circle. Usually ten to fifteen people participate in the dance. One person beats the circle and two others recite a poem or recite a rubai, which is at the beginning of the maqam.
